Hi! I'm an environmental researcher and visual artist based in Jaödeogë/Pittsburgh and Tkaronto/Toronto. I'm originally from Gyeonggi, Korea, but my family immigrated twice—first to Singapore, then to Coast Salish Territories/Vancouver. I've also lived in Tiohtià:ke/Montreal as an undergrad at McGill University, where I studied Honours Mechanical Engineering and spent my summers setting fire to aluminum dust suspensions (for science).

I'm currently doing a PhD at Carnegie Mellon University's Department of Engineering and Public Policy, advised by Professors Valerie Karplus and Nicholas Muller. My work investigates the climate change, air quality, and environmental justice impacts of industrial decarbonization pathways, focusing on high-energy-demand industries such as iron and steel production and data centers. I became interested in industrial decarbonization through my time in engineering consulting, where I worked on design and process solutions for the mining and metals industry and spent time at steel and nickel plants in Canada, Asia, and Central/South America doing site work.

I've been making art since long before I even knew what engineering was. Painting has always been my emotional outlet through many an existential crisis, but recently I've been turning to photography as an art form to address many of the same questions I research for my PhD: how do we express the intangible effects of modernization and industrialization on society? How can we go about trying to balance the immediate benefits of modern systems and technologies with their ineffable but undeniable human and environmental costs, especially when climate and pollution damages will affect some people much more than others based on race, nationality, or wealth?
